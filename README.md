# study-of-cheetah-poop

A (professional, slightly cheeky) field-study repository for the educational investigation of cheetah scat (droppings, feces). This project collects observational protocols, morphology notes, habitat and diet clues, safety and ethics guidance, and an image gallery to support non-invasive biological study and teaching.

---

## Overview

This repository documents best practices and notes for identifying, recording, and interpreting cheetah scat in the field. It is intended for:
- Field biologists and wildlife students doing observational work.
- Educators building lesson plans about carnivore ecology and non-invasive sampling.
- Citizen scientists contributing photographic observations and metadata.

The approach is strictly non-invasive: photos, descriptive notes, and minimal sample collection with proper permits and biosecurity procedures.

---

## Why it matters

Understanding scat helps answer ecological questions without needing to capture animals:
- Diet composition: prey species, relative importance of mammals, birds, or insects.
- Health status: parasites or abnormal contents can indicate disease.
- Spatial ecology: scat locations reveal movement patterns, territorial behavior, and marking sites.
- Conservation monitoring: non-invasive sampling supports population and dietary studies with minimal disturbance.

Good scat data supplements camera traps, telemetry, and observational sightings to build a fuller picture of cheetah ecology.

## Morphology notes

When recording morphology, use standardized fields and photographic scales (ruler/coin) where safe and permitted.

Key morphological descriptors:
- Shape: cylindrical, tapered, segmented. Note whether ends are pointed or blunt.
- Size: approximate length and diameter (record metric measurements). Example fields: length_mm, diameter_mm.
- Color/consistency: fresh vs. aged (moist, crumbly, powdery); color may reflect diet (e.g., dark from recent meat consumption).
- Contents: visible hair, bone fragments, feathers, insect chitin, plant material.
- Smell: pungent/fresh vs. faint (note with caution and never intentionally inhale).
- Associated marks: nearby scrape marks, urine stains, or tracks.

Notes and caveats:
- Morphology varies with age, hydration, and diet; use multiple metrics rather than one indicator.
- When possible, photograph from multiple angles with scale and GPS-enabled metadata.

## Habitat / Diet clues

Use scat to infer recent behavior and habitat usage. Record contextual metadata: GPS, habitat type (savanna, scrub, riverine), time, weather, and nearby signs (kills, tracks).

Dietary indicators:
- Hair and bone fragments → mammalian prey (identify hair microstructure or bone types when safe/authorized).
- Feathers → avian prey.
- Insect remains or large seed fragments → secondary ingestion or opportunistic feeding.
- Plant material or grass → incidental ingestion or self-medication (grazing behavior is occasionally observed).

Habitat clues:
- Scat found on prominent perches (anthills, termite mounds) may indicate scent-marking locations.
- Proximity to water sources or game trails can reveal hunting zones.
- Repeated deposits at one site can indicate territorial boundaries or resting sites.

Combine scat evidence with camera-trap or observational data for robust inferences.

## Ethics / Safety

This work must prioritize safety, ethics, and minimal disturbance.

Field ethics:
- Never follow animals closely to obtain samples or photos.
- Avoid interfering with dens, kills, or denning females with cubs.
- Obtain necessary permits for sample collection and export; follow local and national regulations.

Biosafety and personal protection:
- Wear disposable gloves when handling scat or tools.
- Use sterile tools for any collection; place samples in sealed, labeled containers.
- Decontaminate equipment and wash hands thoroughly after fieldwork.
- Avoid aerosol-generating actions (e.g., sniffing samples).
- Be aware of zoonotic risks (parasites, bacteria) and follow institutional biosafety guidance.

Data ethics:
- When sharing georeferenced data, consider sensitivity: avoid publishing precise locations of dens or vulnerable populations in public repositories.
- Attribute contributions (citizen scientists, local guides) fairly.
